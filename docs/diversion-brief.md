# Diversion

The central idea of the game is this: you are a human living in a distant, post-singularity world where a very small number of surviving people live a life of infinite luxury, immortal. You are dissatisfied with this and think it might be interesting to return the world to how it used to be, when there was suffering and death. Your AI puts your favourite living quarters into a temporal bubble and creates a machine which can modify the past. When you first enter, you realise the past is entirely frozen - but you can interact with it. By placing items in particular places, opening doors, pushing people over, you can create a change to the timeline. When you return, to you can see the effects outside your bubble. You unlock other places to visit as things don't seem to go your way. Then there's some denouement, probably to do with the fact that this was all a diversion by your AI and you were never actually changing the past. 

## narrative things
- the AI is the IF narrator - its tone is obsequious and overly positive. But you get the feeling it's limiting you. 
- examining your house shows lots of interesting things about you and the world you're from. Examining things beyond the bubble shows the world changing. Perhaps you have a telescope or something like that. 
- no NPCs to talk to! But you'd read letters on envelopes, things people were writing or typing, etc
- there develops a story of what happened in history to lead to the singularity

## gameplay things
- accessing areas and unlocking things in order to learn more about what you need to change
- moving objects around - literally picking up things and placing them elsewhere
- fixing broken things
- leaving messages
- returning to places when the changes don't appear to have done anything - it's only examining outside the bubble that tells you you were right (or get to progress), with the AI allowing another place to go back to
- taking things from certain time periods, then carrying them into other time periods
	- taking things from your house and carrying them into other time periods
	- we'd want some limitation on carrying things through the time machine 
- an example: you read letters and notes on a guy's desk and realise he goes into seclusion after his wife left him and creates some tech. It's because he didn't get her a birthday present or some such - you create something and put it on her dressing table. Thus you stop the tech. 

## things to think about
- how does the telescope work, mechanically?
- what are the time machine carry limitations? I like organic matter only. It rarely comes up, but you are completely naked. 
- how do consequences work? There should be a range of things you can effect, all not intended other than one. This is the solution, and you unlock another time period. Everything else is no effect. Real beauty in the design would be the continuation of non-necessary things in later periods (like accidentally creating a religion). 
- How many time periods? Probably five, as three is too few. We'd be ratcheting up the tension as each one doesn't seem to fully do what's needed. You can revisit time periods, and they are changed based on actions in previous periods. The first might be the 1940s, the next the 2350s, then back to Ancient Egypt, return to the 1940s one, then 3290s, etc. So only five time periods, five sets of rooms, but a path for the player through these as they futilely make their changes. 
- what kind of person is the player? Their interiority and motivations for this time machine project are all discoverable in the house - probably a good place to start (as well as hiding things that might be useful to take back with your - thus providing gameplay reason for exploring the narrative)
- what is the AIs agenda? The ai has been designed to give people diversion. So this is all a story - unknown if true or not. Does it matter? You were entertained. What matters is that it has kept a person entertained. META!
